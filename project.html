What are the factors that affect Life Expectancy in different countries?

Introduction

Life Expectancy is an important measure of a population's general health and well-being. It reflects the efficiency of public health initiative, socioeconomic factors, and health care systems. Life Expectancy varies between different countries due to things like improved lifestyle, easy access to healthcare system, and general quality of life. In this project, we will analyze a diverse array of factors that might affect life expectancy, by examining trends over time and comparing across regions and income levels, we seek to uncover patterns, correlations, and disparities that contribute to differences in life expectancy.

The following colorpleth map provides a powerful overview of global health disparities by visualizing the spatial distribution of important health indicators such as life expectancy, adult mortality, and infant deaths. Economic Impact vs. Life Expectancy Bubble Chart

In the following bubble chart, we try to explore the relationship between life expectancy and socioeconomic factors like (GDP, income composition of resources, and schooling). each bubble represents a country, the size of the bubble demonstrates the size of the population of that country, x-Axis is the life expectancy and y-Axis is the selected parameter. the user can select the year from the dropdown list, and hovering over the bubble will show additional and specific data. we can say in general that if a country have a higher socioeconomic parameter, it can have a higher life expectancy. but that is not always the case.


Life Expectancy Disparity: Developing vs. Developed Countries

The huge disparity in life expectancy between "developing" and "developed" continents is illustrated by this box plot. The distribution of life expectancy values for the chosen year within these two categories is shown by each box. Each box's margins indicate the interquartile range (IQR), while the central line shows the median life expectancy. Exceptions are highlighted by outliers that go beyond the whiskers. We can say that developed continents generally have higher average life expectancies and lower variability in life expectancy between the first and third quartiles, with minimum and maximum life expectancies also being higher than in developing countries
